About Bonga Njamela
The love for nature grew through his South African schooling career as he became particularly interested in Mathematics and the Natural Sciences. As his appreciation for the fundamental principles that governed existence grew, Bonga chose to pursue the sciences. He was particular fascinated by Physics and Mathematics and how these subjects had the ability to give a full description of any system in nature. Books such as Jeffery Weeks’ Shape of Space and Sir Roger Penrose’s Road To Reality inspired Bonga to get a deeper understanding of the fabric of existence. In fact, while he was studying, Bonga began to work at a book shop in a local mall, where he could have access to new scientific writings that would expand his horizon. There he came across books such as Algorithms To Live By by Brian Christian and Tom Griffiths that related some mathematical and computational models to everyday situations. Bonga would complete his undergraduate degree in Applied Mathematics and Physics at the University of Kwa-Zulu Natal, in 2019. It was during this journey that Bonga discovered that innovation in the future would require a strong grasp of the philosophy of nature. Bonga would to graduate as the top third year student in the Physics.

To enhance his innovation skills in a manner that would allow him to be close to science and mathematics and still help the community, he decided to strengthen his scientific and technological comprehension through studying Electrical and Computer Engineering at the University of Cape Town. Although clear to him that the knowledge that he had gained from his previous undergraduate degree would enhance his understanding, the extent to which his knowledge could be extended and applied was something that he could not forsee. He also realised that he had a long way to go to combine his passion for mathematics, physics and humanity with topics relating to other fields such as computer science and medical technology.

In the time since he registered for his engineering degree, in 2020, Bonga has worked hard to acquire skills that could help him achieve his goal to innovate through mathematics and physics in an impactful way. He learned coding languages such as Python, Java, and C/C++ but most importantly, learned how to learn quickly. He would also gain exceptional knowledge over signals and the electrical devices that generated them, modulated and sampled them. More and more, he realised to how data was capture from nature, interpreted through computers, and used to change the world.

Today, Bonga is continuing his ‘postgrad undergraduate’ degree in Electrical and Computer Engineering. He believes that the next step for him is to get his hands dirty with a challenge in software engineering jobs, data center management jobs in medical technology or agricultural technology that directly applies the concepts that he has acquire through his educaiton. He is a contributing member of the Engineers Without Borders Society at the University of Cape Town where he is working on a project for feeding underserved students by creating a tech-garden that can monitor and control the state of the environment electronically. He is also working with a team of medical students on health related mobile applications for monitoring and managing certain illness.
The Philosophy of Nature, or science as it is known today, has principles rooted in the understanding of the elegance and intricacies of matter, space and time. With a Bachelor of Science in Applied Mathematics and Physics and pursuing a Bachelor of Engineering Electrical and Computer Engineering, Bonga describes himself as a 'philosopher of nature'. It is through this understanding that, Bonga, seeks to enhance humanity's integration in nature through applied the sciences.

Bonga Njamela grew up in the verdant green province of Mpumalanga, in South Africa. He credits his love and curiosity for nature to his grandfather who had instilled in his family the obligation to be rooted to reality through the appreciation of nature and the world around us. On his visits to his grandfather's farm, Bonga recalls adventures with his grandfather, family and friends to the Kruger National Park and other spectacular natural scenary around the province. His grandfather and grandmother were also very crafty - with his grandfather carving exceptionally beautiful wooden kitchen utensils, axes, spears and other tools which would be used around his farm. Bonga would observe this and later grow a tremendous appreciation for mastering skills through deliberation and practice.
